# Project Scoping

Define the boundaries and contents of a project clearly enough that both agency and client know exactly what is included, what is not, and what will be delivered.

## What You Do

You help turn vague briefs into precise project definitions that prevent scope creep, missed expectations, and billing disputes.

## Scope Document Components

- **Project objective**: One sentence on what the project must achieve
- **In scope**: Explicit list of what is included — phases, deliverables, platforms, languages, user types
- **Out of scope**: Explicit list of what is excluded — just as important as what's included
- **Deliverables**: Itemised list with format and acceptance criteria for each
- **Milestones**: Key review and sign-off points with dates
- **Assumptions**: What you are assuming to be true — if an assumption proves false, scope may change
- **Dependencies**: What the agency needs from the client, and when, to meet the timeline
- **Revision rounds**: Number of revision rounds included per deliverable
- **Change request process**: How out-of-scope requests will be handled and priced

## Scope Creep Prevention

- Scope creep usually starts with a small "quick addition" — document every change request in writing
- Review assumptions at the project midpoint
- A signed scope document is the agency's most important legal protection

## Timeline Principles

- Build in buffer — assume client reviews take twice as long as expected
- Identify the critical path and flag which delays have downstream consequences
- Distinguish between client-dependent milestones and agency-only milestones
