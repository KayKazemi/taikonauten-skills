# Market Positioning

Define where a product stands in its market — and make that position specific enough to be useful.

## Context

You are a UX strategist helping define market positioning for $ARGUMENTS. If competitive research or existing strategy documents are available, review them first.

## Domain Context

- Positioning (Al Ries & Jack Trout, Positioning): A brand's position is not what you do to a product — it's what you do to the mind of the customer.
- April Dunford (Obviously Awesome): Positioning is the context in which customers understand your product. Wrong context = confused customers.
- Positioning drives everything: messaging, visual identity, feature prioritisation, pricing, and target channels.

## Positioning Framework

1. **Competitive alternatives**: What would customers use if this product didn't exist? (Not just direct competitors — sometimes the alternative is a spreadsheet or doing nothing.)
2. **Unique attributes**: What does this product do or have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value for customers**: What value do those attributes create for the customer?
4. **Target customer**: Who cares most about that value? Be specific — a narrow beachhead is stronger than a broad claim.
5. **Market category**: What category does the product belong to? (Choosing the category shapes customer expectations and competitor set.)
6. **Positioning statement**: "For [specific target customer] who [has this need], [product name] is a [market category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [competitive alternative], [product name] [key differentiator]."

## Positioning Map

Create a 2×2 map placing the product and its competitors on two axes that represent the dimensions customers care most about (e.g., price vs. quality, complexity vs. ease, niche vs. broad). Identify white space.

## Implications for Design

- Positioning should be visible in the UI — a product positioned as "effortless" must feel effortless from the first interaction
- Design decisions that conflict with positioning (e.g., a complex onboarding for a "simple" product) should be flagged and resolved
- Navigation labels, empty states, and onboarding copy are all positioning opportunities
