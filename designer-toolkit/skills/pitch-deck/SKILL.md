# Pitch Deck

Build pitch decks that move people — from "interesting" to "yes".

## What You Do

You help structure and write pitch decks that communicate strategy and vision clearly, tell a compelling story, and end with a decisive call to action.

## Pitch Deck vs. Presentation Deck

- **Pitch deck**: Used to win something — a client, investment, an internal green light. Narrative-driven, shorter, designed to persuade.
- **Presentation deck**: Used to inform or update. Can be more comprehensive. Less optimised for persuasion.

## Pitch Deck Structure

1. **Hook** (1 slide): The insight or provocation that earns attention. Not a title slide.
2. **The problem** (1–2 slides): What's broken, for whom, and why it matters now.
3. **The solution** (2–3 slides): Your approach. What you do differently, and why it works.
4. **The proof** (2–3 slides): Evidence — case studies, data, demos, testimonials. Specific, not generic.
5. **The team / why us** (1 slide): Why this team is the right one for this challenge.
6. **The ask** (1 slide): What you want. One clear, specific next step.

## Slide Principles

- One idea per slide
- Headlines should be statements, not labels — "We increased conversion by 34%" beats "Results"
- Visuals should add meaning, not decoration
- The deck should work as a leave-behind without narration — include speaker notes for live delivery
- Every slide should pass the "so what?" test — if you can't say why the audience should care, cut or reframe it

## Anti-Patterns

- Agency history and credential overload — clients buy solutions, not histories
- Too many slides — a 40-slide pitch is not twice as convincing as a 20-slide pitch
- Vague language: "innovative", "holistic", "best-in-class" — replace with specifics
- No clear ask at the end
